Overview

Understanding Earth's Critical Zone

Understanding the process interactions and feedbacks among water, porous geological media, microbes, and vascular plants is crucial for predicting how Earth's critical zone will respond to future climatic conditions. Yet the integrated co-evolution of landscapes under change is notoriously difficult to investigate.

Laboratory studies are limited in spatial and temporal scale, while field studies lack observational density and control. To bridge the gap between controlled laboratory and uncontrolled field studies, the University of Arizona built a macrocosm experiment of unprecedented scale: the Landscape Evolution Observatory (LEO) at Biosphere 2.

Insights

Linkages & Feedbacks

Initial sensor, sampler, and soil-coring data are providing insights into the linkages between water flow, weathering, and biological community development. These interacting processes drive the model systems to increasingly complex states — potentially perturbed by changes in climatic forcing.

Hydrological Flow Paths

Tracking how water moves through initially homogeneous basalt — forming preferential flow networks, triggering weathering fronts, and emerging as seepage at the slope base.

Geochemical Weathering

Measuring real-time mineral dissolution and secondary mineral formation as water, CO₂, and biological activity transform bare rock into evolving soil.

Microbial Community Assembly

Tracking how microbial communities colonize virgin substrate — and how their metabolic activity feeds back into weathering rates and nutrient cycling.

Vascular Plant Integration

After abiotic initialization, vascular plants are introduced — testing how root systems accelerate weathering and reorganize subsurface hydrology.

By intensively monitoring the evolutionary trajectory, integrating data with mathematical models, and fostering community-wide collaborations, LEO aims to predict the coupled hydro-biogeochemical and ecological responses of Earth systems to global change.

— Dr. Scott Saleska, Director of LEO Research
