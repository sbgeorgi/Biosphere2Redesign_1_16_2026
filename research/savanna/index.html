Overview

A Hydrological Bridge

The savanna biome was designed to perform several functions within Biosphere 2. Its primary goal was to provide a hydrological transition zone between the desert and rainforest mesocosms — learning how to balance atmospheric chemistry by varying hydrological regimes within the tropical mesocosms.

The savanna was designed as a scaled-down model of tropical savannas, duplicating ecosystem processes while capturing essential features of biodiversity and aesthetics. Vegetation zones were created primarily from edible species, including acacias for galagos, large-seeded grasses for finches, and fruit-bearing trees for birds and primates.

Geography

Four Major Regions

The savanna is divided into four major regions: Quartzite Slope, Upper Savanna, Granite Slope, and Lower Savanna — each with distinct geology, hydrology, and plant communities.

Quartzite Slope

The northernmost section, constructed from quartzite boulders — a rocky substrate setting the upland boundary of the savanna.

Upper Savanna

Three vegetation sections including Everglades freshwater wetland species, an Acacia-dominated gallery forest, and billabong depressions.

Billabongs

Depressions designed to flood periodically — creating hyperseasonal habitats that alternate between waterlogged and desiccated, favoring grasses over trees.

Lower Savanna

Southernmost section adjacent to upper thornscrub, dominated by Brachiaria mutica grass canopy.

Hyperseasonal environments alternate between waterlogged and desiccated conditions — killing most trees and favoring grasses. The billabongs of the Biosphere 2 savanna let us study this underrepresented ecosystem dynamic in a controlled setting.

— Biosphere 2 Research Team

Savanna Research

The savanna biome supports research into tropical grassland ecology, hydrological cycling, hyperseasonal plant dynamics, and food-web interactions. Proposals welcome through the user facility program.
